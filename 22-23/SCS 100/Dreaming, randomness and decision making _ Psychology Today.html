Dreaming, randomness and decision making

How dreams facilitate decision making

Posted May 27, 2013

  • Share
  • Tweet
  • Share
  • Email

.

In his recent book “The luck of the draw: The role of lotteries in decision making” (Oxford University Press, 2011), Peter Stone discusses when it is appropriate to randomly assign goods to persons or entities with equally strong claims on the goods. For justice to win out in such cases the decision must be made without bias. The decision must be made in an impartial manner. That is why the Goddess of Justice is depicted with a blindfold in the ancient world.

To operate blindly or without bias, it is necessary to use random assignment or lotteries to make the decision on fair allocation of goods when all parties have equally strong claims to those goods.

Stone’s discussion becomes fascinating when he attempts to generalize the rules under which lotteries can help us arrive at optimal decisions given seemingly insoluble alternatives. Interestingly, Stone argues that lotteries are the ideal way to make decisions when “reasons” do not work. When rational arguments concerning the issues cannot adequately address the issues or would make things worse if allowed to inform the decision, then you need a mechanism to exclude reasons in the decision making process. Lotteries provide that mechanism. They, as Stone puts it, “filter out” reasons or “sanitize” the decision making process.

Think of all the situations in your life where you have been confronted with a situation and you have tried all the reasonable solutions and none have worked so you are left with wanting to at least avoid the bad solutions. When the good reasons run out and there is still a chance that you can avoid the bad reasons you turn to drawing lots.

article continues after advertisement

There are millions of situations where we have to act whether or not we have good reasons for doing so. We would like to do so while avoiding the bad solutions. There are all kinds of situations where we act on gut instinct or by consulting the heart rather than the head. These likely include all the things that really matter to us such as how best to raise the kids, how to choose a mate, how to navigate social situations, how to make and spend money and how to choose friends.

When facing these sorts of complex decisions that have no clear easy answers we can procrastinate and hope for new info on which to base a rational decision, but in many cases waiting does not work and no amount of further reasoning will help. In these sorts of situations drawing lots will yield a decision that will minimize chances of proceeding on the basis of bad reasoning alone.

If Stone is right that lotteries work by filtering out operations based on bad reasons, then use of a lottery is not irrational but a-rational. But what does all this have to do with dreams?

When I am confronting a complex problem, particularly one that involves me emotionally or one that involves significant risk or high stakes I try to think through all the ramifications I can and then make a decision. But there are times when all the thinking about a problem gives me no clear way forward and then I resort to gut instinct and the like. But what exactly does this mean? I do not consult the flutters in my stomach when making these decisions. I do not examine my emotions and then, finding them to be in upheaval make a decision based on them. I do not, as the ancients did, cut open animals or consult astrologers to make decisions…so what do I do in such cases? What do we all do in such cases? I suggest we turn the problem over to the lottery mechanism in our brains…and that mechanism we call dreams…in short we sleep on it!

But how might dreams acts like a lottery mechanism in decision making? If Stone is correct about the functional mechanism of lotteries in decision making, the first thing dreams would have to do is exclude computational processes that produce conventional “reasons”. We have known for some time now that REM sleep activation is characterized by inhibition of prefrontal activity. Prefrontal systems very likely mediate reasoning processes that require working memory resources. Inhibition of this activity would likely shut down access to this sort of reasoning and its products. In addition, dreams would have to re-present those situations that escape easy solutions. These situations would be emotionally complex affairs involving risk with no easy answers. Here desires clash with seemingly insoluble realities. The “plots” or story lines of many dreams fit this scenario as anyone can attest who has remembered their dreams. If dreams do perform such functions then we must conclude they play a crucial role in decision making and that if true would be big news.
